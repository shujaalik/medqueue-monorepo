Abstract
This abstract provides a high-level technical synopsis of the MedQueue system, em-
phasizing the operational problem context in Pakistani outpatient clinics, the proposed
browser-native architecture, and the major workflow outcomes (real-time queue synchro-
nization, role-based dashboards, and proactive messaging). The content below is kept
exactly as originally authored and is presented as the official abstract for this report.
Despite advances in technology, many clinics still depend on outdated paper
systems. These old methods slow things down. Long waits follow. Patient
care gets delayed. Triage becomes disorganized. Satisfaction drops. One
solution comes forward: MedQueue. This tool shifts operations online. It
organizes appointments digitally. Instead of handing out slips, staff assign
virtual spots. Information updates live across screens. The design emerged
through iterative testing cycles. Developers used current coding standards.
Frameworks were chosen for responsiveness. Structure improved step by
step. Clutter fades when displays guide movement clearly. Order forms
where confusion once lived.
Despite no app needed, MedQueue sends automatic updates straight to
phones using built-in messaging systems like SMS and WhatsApp. Because
of this setup, people receive estimated wait times without downloading
extra software. Patients stay informed from anywhere, reducing pressure
on waiting rooms. Clinic hallways clear out naturally when access shifts
outside the building. Updates flow continuously, making check-ins less
disruptive. Control moves into the hands of those receiving care.
Despite its clean look, behind the scenes MedQueue captures full patient
journeys in detail, logging each step from first check-in to last clinician entry.
Permissions shift by role, giving staff only what they need - receptionists
see one view, clinicians another. Dashboards stay focused because access
does too. Data flows into structured logs automatically, reducing gaps. Each
action becomes part of a larger record meant for analysis later. The system
shapes itself around who is using it, adjusting quietly without extra steps.
Tracking runs continuously, feeding insights back into daily operations.
One main goal shapes this effort - offering low-cost, adaptable systems
powered by smart technology, built for smaller clinics and doctors’ offices in

Beginning with how patients move through clinics, digital healthcare trans-

Executive Summary
The executive summary expands on the abstract by outlining (i) the context and urgency
of the operational problem, (ii) the key goals and system capabilities of MedQueue, (iii)
the overall development methodology and implementation posture, and (iv) the strategic
value and expected impact for small-to-medium clinics in Pakistan. The numbered
points below are preserved exactly and are supplemented by the surrounding structure
for formal report presentation.
1. Context and Crisis
Despite growing demands, health centers across Pakistan struggle under outdated sys-
tems. Rather than streamlining processes, paper-based check-ins deepen inefficiencies.
Long lines form because handwritten records cannot keep pace. Without digital tools,
managing patients becomes unmanageable. Disorder grows where organization should
prevail.
What clogs up administration isn’t just inefficiency - it chips away at how well patients
get cared for. Buried under inconsistent records, staff at intake struggle to keep track,
which feeds mistakes and drags out sign-ins. At the same time, doctors pull focus from
exams to answer the same questions about delays, again and again. When information
stays stuck or unclear, stress builds among those waiting - mood darkens, patience wears
thin. This steady pressure degrades each visit while straining those delivering care.
2. Goals and the MedQueue Change
One answer to long-standing problems in healthcare systems lies in affordable, adaptable
digital tools - tools that upgrade patient flow without straining limited budgets. What
follows describes MedQueue, a solution built precisely for this gap. Instead of paper
slips or manual check-ins, it uses a streamlined online interface where appointments
align smoothly across departments. Designed for ease, the system runs through standard
browsers, removing the need for special hardware or complex setup.
MedQueue aims first at steadier hospital routines and clearer operations. Starting with
sign-in and ending at release, automated handling of patients moves care from hunch-
based responses to planning guided by live information. With this technology in place,
staff perform expert tasks more smoothly, paperwork loads shrink fast, while tension
common in clinic queues fades step by step.
3. How systems work and what they’re built like

Starting off differently each time, MedQueue meets goals using linked features built
around how care actually works nearby. These functions adapt step by step to match real
conditions on the ground. Instead of copying global models, they reflect regional needs.
Each piece connects smoothly to the next. Progress happens through alignment, not
force. Functionality grows from daily use, not theory. Requirements evolve as clinics
change. Design follows practice, not the reverse
Starting with arrival, patients get logged instantly by staff using one shared online
platform - no paper forms touch the counter. A separate digital token appears right away,
locking their place securely in line through automatic assignment. This process runs
without delays, replacing old check-in methods entirely.
Each person sees only what matters to their job. Depending on who logs in, the interface
shifts automatically. Staff at reception handle intake details using one layout. Doctors
and therapists get another view entirely - one showing just their scheduled patients.
Information stays separated by design. That way, no one accesses records outside their
scope of work. Clarity comes from limits built into navigation paths. Workflow runs
smoother when screens match responsibilities.
Throughout clinics, live visual trackers adjust instantly when people move between
consultation stages. Screens placed inside waiting areas show current line positions
without noise. Updates travel straight to personal phones using regular messaging
services like SMS or WhatsApp. These alerts include expected wait times, built from
ongoing movement patterns. No extra software installation becomes necessary for
access. Information flows continuously across platforms, keeping everyone informed as
situations change.
Unexpected demands shape how the system adjusts - software supports sudden shifts
in patient order when emergencies arise. Though routines exist, priority changes slip
through seamlessly, guided by real-time needs rather than fixed rules. Behind the scenes,
structure remains stable even as individual paths shift abruptly. Decisions made under
pressure fit within preset boundaries, preserving overall function while bending for
urgency.
4. How things are built who builds them and where they matter
Beginning with rough sketches, MedQueue took shape through repeated sprints under
Agile Scrum rules. Because each phase built on the last, progress moved steadily forward
without long delays. Testing happened constantly, not just at the end, which helped
catch flaws early. User insights were folded into updates quickly, thanks to short review
intervals. Built with lean modern tools for the web, it runs smoothly even on older
machines. So clinics with tight budgets in cities like Lahore or Quetta face fewer barriers

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Healthcare delivery in outpatient departments (OPDs) is shaped not only by clinical
expertise but also by the operational mechanics that determine how quickly, fairly,
and safely patients move through reception, triage, consultation, and discharge. In
Pakistan, small-to-medium clinics constitute a large portion of first-contact medical
access; however, many such facilities still depend on hand-written registers, physical
tokens, and ad-hoc verbal calling mechanisms. These practices create a bottleneck at
the exact point where a clinic should be most predictable: patient intake and waiting
management.
From an informatics perspective, outpatient operations can be viewed as a real-time
service system where arrivals are stochastic, service times vary by clinician and case
complexity, and perceived fairness strongly impacts user satisfaction. When queue states
are opaque, perceived waiting time increases, the likelihood of repeated interruptions
rises, and overall throughput is reduced. The MedQueue project is positioned as an
applied software engineering response to this operational reality, designed to introduce
visibility, predictability, and role-appropriate control into everyday clinic workflows.
1.2
Problem Statement
Outpatient clinics in Pakistan frequently experience overcrowded waiting areas and
unpredictable patient calling order due to manual, paper-based processes that provide
no reliable real-time visibility into queue state. As a result, patients experience height-
ened anxiety and dissatisfaction, reception staff are repeatedly interrupted for status
inquiries, and clinicians face operational delays when patients miss their turn or when
emergencies require rapid reprioritization. There exists a practical need for an affordable,
browser-based queue management system that supports walk-in patient handling, role-
based workflows, remote status visibility, and proactive notifications without requiring
expensive kiosk hardware or mandatory mobile app installation.
1

---PAGE BREAK---
1.3
Objectives
The primary objective of MedQueue is to design and implement a low-overhead, browser-
native queue management platform that digitizes outpatient waiting workflows while
remaining feasible for adoption in small-to-medium clinical settings. Concretely, the
project aims to: (i) replace manual token issuance with a controlled digital token lifecycle,
(ii) provide synchronized, real-time queue visibility across staff and patient views, (iii)
reduce patient uncertainty through proactive alerts (e.g., WhatsApp/SMS), (iv) enforce
role-based access control so that each stakeholder sees only what is necessary for their
tasks, and (v) enable operational learning through structured logs and analytics.
1.4
Scope
The scope of this report covers the end-to-end software engineering lifecycle of
MedQueue as a Final Year Design Project (FYDP): background and rationale, com-
petitor and literature grounding, requirement elicitation, system design and prototype
specification, implementation details of the queue engine and notification triggers,
and validation through testing and evaluation artifacts. The system scope is centered
on outpatient queue handling (walk-in registration, token sequencing, consultation
progression, basic analytics, and notifications). It does not attempt to replace full
hospital information systems (HIS) or electronic medical record (EMR) suites; instead,
it targets the operational gap between appointment booking tools and on-ground waiting
management.
1.5
Significance of the Project
MedQueue is significant because it reframes waiting as an information and coordination
problem rather than an unavoidable inconvenience. By shifting queue state into a real-
time digital artifact, clinics can reduce crowd density, improve perceived fairness, and
support calmer patient experiences. At the same time, the system decreases administra-
tive interruption load on staff and enables data-informed decisions for clinic management.
As a locally grounded solution that leverages communication channels already dominant
in Pakistan (such as WhatsApp and SMS), MedQueue also demonstrates how practical
digital health interventions can be designed for adoption realism rather than idealized
infrastructure assumptions.

Despite global advances in medicine, many clinics across Pakistan still depend on
outdated paper-based systems to manage appointments. Efficiency in moving patients

Objectives
Digitization and Modernization of Outpatient Queue Structures
The foremost operational imperative of the MedQueue framework is the systematic
and complete digitization of legacy patient queue structures. Historically, outpatient
facilities have operated under a physical token architecture, which is inherently limited
by geographical constraints, human processing errors, and a total lack of transparency.
By engineering a migration away from these paper-based tokens, the system facilitates
a profound institutional shift in how administrative data is captured, preserved, and
distributed.
This structural transformation is achieved through several core operational developments:
• Cloud-Native Centralization and Global Accessibility: By leveraging a cloud-
native architecture, data access is decentralized. Information is no longer confined
to a single, handwritten ledger at the front reception desk. Instead, critical data
paths open up across a distributed network, allowing secure, multi-tenant access
from any authorized browser-native node.
• Synchronized Real-Time Data Streams: The platform replaces static, manual
updates with automated, real-time telemetry. The moment a patient is logged
into the database, an entry is generated across the entire system. Updates flow
seamlessly to all connected interfaces, establishing an immediate data loop. This
ensures that a sequence change initiated by a front-desk receptionist is instantly
mirrored on the physician's dashboard and public-facing signage modules without
a time delay.
• Elimination of Physical Documentation Intermediaries: By connecting insti-
tutional systems without physical slips, the clinic eliminates material overhead,
printing equipment failures, and the administrative mess of lost or misread tickets.
Information moves dynamically alongside the patient's physical transit through
the clinic lifecycle—from initial entry, through triage and medical examination, to
final checkout.
• Cross-Device Omnichannel Dissemination: Because the interface is designed to
scale dynamically, real-time updates appear instantly across an array of consumer
devices, ranging from large waiting room displays to personal mobile phones.
All stakeholders—including administrative clerks, senior medical officers, and
patients—possess immediate visibility into the queue sequence.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
2.1
Purpose and Organization of the Chapter
This chapter situates MedQueue within the broader research and practice landscape
of queue management systems (QMS), patient flow optimization, and low-cost
digital health interventions. The objective is to establish (i) what is already known
about waiting behavior and service system design, (ii) which solutions exist in the
market and research literature, and (iii) what gap remains specifically relevant to
Pakistani outpatient clinics where infrastructure, budgets, and user habits differ
from high-resource environments.
The chapter proceeds from foundational theory to applied systems. It begins with
a conceptual grounding in queueing theory and the psychology of waiting, then
reviews the dominant categories of existing solutions (hardware-centric token
systems, appointment/doctor discovery platforms, and SaaS virtual waitlists).
Finally, it synthesizes findings to identify the specific design opportunity addressed
by MedQueue.
2.2
Theoretical Foundations
2.2.1
Queueing Theory in Outpatient Operations
At its core, outpatient waiting can be modeled as a stochastic service system where
patient arrivals are uncertain and service times vary based on case complexity,
clinician style, and operational disruptions. Queueing theory provides a vocabulary
for expressing these dynamics in terms of arrival rates, service rates, utilization,
waiting time distributions, and fairness policies. In practical clinic environments,
a strict theoretical model (e.g., a perfectly stationary
M/M/1
queue) rarely holds; however, the theory remains valuable because it clarifies
trade-offs. For example, higher utilization increases throughput efficiency but
typically increases the expected waiting time and the variability of waits. Systems
25

---PAGE BREAK---
like MedQueue are therefore designed not only to improve the physical flow, but
also to improve the informational flow that allows stakeholders to make better
decisions under variability.
2.2.2
Psychology of Waiting and Perceived Delay
A consistent finding in service management literature is that perceived waiting
time can be as important as, and sometimes more influential than, actual waiting
time. When patients lack information (e.g., not knowing their position or how long
remains), anxiety increases and patience decreases, which leads to repeated status
inquiries, arguments over fairness, and escalations that burden staff. Conversely,
even if the objective wait is unchanged, transparency and predictable updates can
reduce uncertainty and therefore reduce perceived delay. This insight motivates
MedQueue’s real-time visibility and proactive alert design.
2.2.3
Patient Flow Optimization and Operational Informatics
Patient flow optimization extends beyond the queue itself to include how patients
transition between stages (registration, triage, consultation, and checkout) and
how information is captured at each step. The operational objective is to minimize
bottlenecks and rework while maintaining safety and privacy. In a digital system,
each action (registration, token assignment, call-next, completion, absent/no-show)
becomes a logged event, which enables both auditing (e.g., emergency override
tracking) and analytics (e.g., peak hour identification). MedQueue leverages this
principle by treating the queue as a continuously updated dataset rather than a
one-time paper record.
2.3
Taxonomy of Existing Solutions
Existing queue-related solutions used in healthcare and other service settings can
be categorized into three dominant approaches:
– Hardware-Centric Token and Signage Systems: These are kiosk and LED
display deployments that produce highly visible, structured waiting rooms
but typically require proprietary equipment and higher capital expenditure.
– Appointment Discovery and Booking Platforms: These platforms optimize
the pre-visit scheduling step (finding a doctor and reserving a time slot) but
often do not manage the live, on-ground queue after arrival.
– Browser-Based Virtual Waitlists (SaaS): These systems introduce remote
26

---PAGE BREAK---
waiting via web links and SMS updates, but may not align with local usage
patterns, cost constraints, or communication channel dominance.
The remainder of this chapter evaluates representative systems within these cate-
gories and relates them to the MedQueue design decisions.
2.4
Introduction
MedQueue is a web-based application aiming to optimize patient flow
in medical clinics by connecting receptionists, doctors, and patients
through a real-time queue management engine. The process begins
when a receptionist registers a patient, generating a digital token. The
patient can then track their queue status re-motely via a mobile dash-
board or WhatsApp notifications. This solution enables clinics to
manage walk-in patients and appointments efficiently, reducing over-
crowding in waiting areas and minimizing patient anxiety. By lever-
aging widely used communication channels, MedQueue transforms a
chaotic manual process into a streamlined, digital experience.
2.5
Related System Analysis/Literature Review
2.5.1
The Problem
Accessing timely healthcare in Pakistan is often overshadowed by
the stress of waiting. In most outpatient clinics (OPDs) and private
practices, patients face a complete lack of transparency regarding their
turn. Traditional methods rely on handwritten slips or verbal calls,
which forces patients to remain glued to the waiting room for hours,
fearing they might miss their name.
This uncertainty leads to overcrowded and stressful clinic environments,
in-
creasing the risk of cross-infection and general anxiety. Receptionists
are con-stantly interrupted by patients asking, ”How much longer?”,
which disrupts ad-ministrative tasks. Furthermore, doctors often face
delays when patients step out and miss their turn, leading to inefficient
slot utilization.
While high-end hospitals use sophisticated Hospital Management Sys-
tems (HMS), these are often too expensive and complex for small-to-
27

---PAGE BREAK---
medium clinics. Conversely, existing doctor booking apps solve the
problem of finding a doctor but do not solve the problem of waiting
once the patient arrives at the clinic. There is a ”missing middle” in
the market for a solution that manages the live, on-ground queue for
walk-in patients without requiring expensive hardware kiosks.
2.5.2
Literature Review
The concept of MedQueue is grounded in Queueing Theory and Pa-
tient Flow Optimization. Research in healthcare management suggests
that ”perceived wait time” is often more detrimental to patient satis-
faction than the actual wait time. When patients are uninformed about
their status, anxiety levels rise significantly. Scholars have examined
the impact of Queue Management Systems (QMS) on service quality.
Studies indicate that providing real-time information reduces psycho-
logical stress and perceived waiting duration (Maister, 1985). Modern
QMS solutions leverage mobile technology to create ”virtual queues,”
allowing customers to wait remotely. This concept, widely successful
in the banking and retail sectors,
is now transitioning into healthcare (Smart Health).
For a digital health intervention to be successful in this region, it must
rely on familiar technologies—such as SMS and WhatsApp—rather
than requiring users to download complex proprietary apps.
MedQueue integrates these findings by focusing on a ”Push Notifica-
tion” model. Instead of forcing patients to constantly check an app,
the system proac-tively notifies them. This aligns with recent trends
in mHealth (Mobile Health), where text-based interventions have
shown high engagement rates in low-resource settings. By combining
cloud-based real-time databases (like Firebase) with ubiq-uitous com-
munication APIs, MedQueue aims to democratize access to efficient
queue management.

Chapter 4
System Design
4.1
Design Goals and Principles
The system design of MedQueue is guided by the requirement that it must operate
in resource-constrained, high-variability outpatient environments while remaining
simple enough to adopt without dedicated IT teams. Accordingly, the design
prioritizes the following principles:
– Browser-Native Accessibility: The system must run in standard browsers
across desktop and mobile devices, reducing deployment friction.
– Real-Time Consistency: All stakeholders should observe the same queue
truth within a short bounded delay, ensuring operational correctness and
perceived fairness.
– Role Isolation and Privacy: Screens and privileges are constrained by role;
public views show only non-identifying queue tokens.
– Low Interaction Cost: High-frequency operations (register patient, call next,
mark absent, emergency override) must be minimal-click, minimal-typing.
– Auditability: Actions that can affect fairness (especially priority overrides)
must be logged to enable accountability.
4.2
High-Level Architecture
MedQueue follows a modular web architecture in which the queue state is repre-
sented as a set of authoritative records in a centralized real-time database. Client
interfaces (receptionist, doctor, admin, patient/public view) subscribe to updates
and render the current state according to the user role.
4.2.1
Major Architectural Components
– Authentication and Role Gateway: Verifies staff identities and maps ac-
counts to role-scoped permissions.
49

---PAGE BREAK---
– Queue Engine Service: Manages token sequencing (FIFO by default),
applies emergency override policies, and updates state transitions.
– Notification Dispatcher: Integrates with WhatsApp/SMS APIs to push
proactive “Get Ready” alerts based on queue position.
– Presentation Layer Clients: Receptionist dashboard for intake, doctor
dashboard for consultation flow, admin dashboard for analytics/configuration,
and patient/public views for queue visibility.
– Analytics and Reporting Pipeline: Aggregates events into operational
metrics (wait time patterns, throughput, cancellations/no-shows).
4.3
Data Design (Conceptual Model)
MedQueue is designed around an evented queue lifecycle. Each patient visit
progresses through states such as registered, waiting, called, serving, completed,
or absent. These state transitions are stored as structured records with timestamps.
This enables:
– real-time rendering of the live queue,
– reproducible calculation of wait-time estimates, and
– later reporting and audit checks on operations.
4.4
Security and Privacy by Design
The system design enforces privacy through separation of views and controlled
disclosure. The public waiting display is intentionally token-based to avoid expos-
ing patient phone numbers or identity details. Role-based access and authenticated
staff sessions ensure that sensitive configuration and analytics capabilities are
available only to authorized personnel.

5.8
Algorithm / Logic Explanation
MedQueue relies on explicit business rules to maintain an orderly,
transparent, and fair operational flow inside the clinic.
5.8.1
First-In-First-Out (FIFO) Queue Sequencing
When a token is generated, the queue engine evaluates the current active
pool and appends the patient sequentially:
– Token Assignment: The engine computes the next incremented sequential
integer (e.g., T-19 to T-20) based on arrival time.
65

---PAGE BREAK---
– Dynamic Wait Time Estimation: The engine calculates estimated re-
maining wait times by multiplying the number of preceding tokens by the
doctor’s average historical consultation duration, updating dynamically if a
session runs over schedule.
5.8.2
Priority Override Logic
To accommodate unexpected clinical emergencies, an override mecha-
nism bypasses standard FIFO rules:
– Insertion: The receptionist can flag an arriving patient as an “Emergency,”
which instantly shifts that token to the absolute top of the active queue.
– Audit Trail: To prevent operational misuse, every single priority override
action is hard-logged and flagged in the database for mandatory Admin
review.
5.8.3
Proactive Notification Trigger Logic
The system automatically tracks position changes to manage patient
dispersion:
– Position Check: The queue service monitors the live index relative to the
active consulting slot.
– Trigger: The moment a patient shift to exactly 3rd in line, an automated
system trigger pings the notification service.
– Alert Outbound: The API fires a “Get Ready” WhatsApp text link to the
patient’s phone, allowing them to return safely to the waiting room from a
remote location.
5.8.4
End-of-Day Archive & Reset Logic
At closing hours, a database cleanup script is run to refresh the system
state:
– Archive: The system collects all data fields from the active queue and pushes
them into historical database tables for analytics.
– Reset: The live active queue array is cleared out, and the token counter is
systematically reset to 1, preparing the clinic profile for the next morning’s
operations.

Chapter 8
Conclusion & Future Work
8.1
Consolidated Contributions
MedQueue contributes a practical, locally deployable queue management approach
for outpatient clinics that sits between two extremes: (i) high-cost, hardware-
heavy enterprise deployments and (ii) informal manual processes that provide no
transparency or analytics. The system demonstrates that clinically meaningful
operational improvement can be achieved through a lightweight browser-first
platform when the design is aligned with local constraints and communication
habits.
From a software engineering standpoint, the project’s key contributions include:
– a real-time queue engine with controlled state transitions and exception
handling (no-show and emergency override),
– role-based access control with separate dashboards for admin, doctor, recep-
tionist, and patient/public views,
– proactive notification triggers using WhatsApp/SMS to reduce crowding and
uncertainty without app installation,
– analytics-oriented logging that enables operational learning over time,
– an integrated AI clinical scribe workflow concept that targets clinician docu-
mentation friction.
8.2
Limitations (Context-Aware)
While the system achieves its intended functional scope, limitations remain tied to
realistic field constraints. Most notably, the reliance on continuous connectivity
impacts real-time synchronization during network dropouts, and recurring mes-
saging API costs may affect long-term operational budgets for very small clinics.
Additionally, successful adoption depends on behavioral alignment: staff must
consistently operate digital controls (e.g., “Call Next”) so that the queue truth
remains accurate.

8.3
Future Roadmap (Implementation-Oriented)
Future work can be structured as a roadmap with clear engineering goals rather
than generic enhancements. High-value directions include offline-first synchro-

input validation to reduce human entry errors, and scaled multi-clinic deployments
with stronger configuration isolation. The goal of future iterations would be to
strengthen reliability, reduce marginal costs, and improve adoption resilience

Conclusion & Future Work
8.4
Conclusion
The development and deployment of MedQueue represent a significant
milestone in modernizing outpatient healthcare operations and address-
ing the psychological stress of “waiting anxiety” within the Pakistani
medical ecosystem. By replacing chaotic paper-and-pen token rou-
tines with a transparent digital queue map, the platform establishes an
affordable and efficient workflow for medical teams and patients alike.
The project followed Agile Scrum practices, organizing development
into short, iterative sprint cycles. This structural methodology allowed
the team to build, test, and refine core modules—such as walk-in check-
ins, WhatsApp alerts, and doctor dashboards—incrementally based on
live feedback, ensuring a highly stable and user-centric final production
release.
8.4.1
System Achievement
MedQueue successfully delivers a unified, device-agnostic web plat-
form that simul-taneously coordinates four distinct user roles (Admin,
Doctor, Receptionist, and Patient) on standard everyday hardware. Key
operational achievements include:
– Real-Time Queue Sync: Automated token generation and dynamic wait
time estimations that self-correct as lines shift.
– AI Clinical Scribe Integration: An innovative “AI Clinical Scribe Co-
Pilot” built directly into the doctor’s portal, which successfully maps

conver-sational dictation or raw shorthand notes into structured diag-
nostic reports and prescriptions.
– Proactive Patient Dispatch: Automated text-link triggers that alert re-mote
patients when they are exactly 3rd in line, effectively decreasing phys-ical
waiting room crowd density.
8.4.2
Validation
Comprehensive QA and system testing were conducted to evaluate
responsiveness, security, and functional integrity. The platform success-
fully met or exceeded all target performance requirements under test
conditions:
– Queue Synchronization Time: Achieved a live refresh rate of 1.0 second

– Standard Page Load Time: Measured at 1.8 seconds (exceeding the target

– Usability Rating: Secured an exceptional average score of 4.7 / 5 across all

Security validation confirmed that role-based restrictions were rigidly
enforced via JWT, and patient privacy was protected by strictly display-

Overall, the evaluation confirms that MedQueue satisfies its primary
objectives, delivering a highly reli-able, cost-effective, and professional
healthcare utility.
8.5
System Limitations and Challenges
Despite passing its core testing phases and meeting all primary func-
tional parame-ters, several operational limitations and technical chal-
lenges were identified during development and system evaluation:
– Internet Dependency and Lack of Offline Mode: Because the applica-tion
relies completely on cloud-based databases (Cloud Firebase/Firestore) to
push live status updates, a continuous internet connection is required. If
92

---PAGE BREAK---
local clinic connectivity drops out, active screen synchronization pauses,
restricting dashboards exclusively to their last known locally cached state.
– Recurring API Communication Costs:
Leveraging familiar, high-
engagement networks like WhatsApp and SMS eliminates complex
application down-loads for users but introduces recurring operational
expenses. Transactional alerts sent through external messaging gateways
incur a per-message cost that scales directly with a clinic’s patient volume,
posing a potential finan-
cial barrier for very small clinics.
– Behavioral Adjustments and Habits: The system demands a cultural shift
in clinic operations. Medical practitioners must form a new habit of actively
interacting with digital dashboard buttons (such as clicking “Call Next” or
“Finish Case”) during rapid clinical pacing rather than calling out names
verbally.
– Digital Literacy Barriers: While younger demographics navigate the re-
mote tracking links seamlessly, older or digitally illiterate patient segments
face accessibility barriers and must continue to rely on manual, verbal assis-
tance from the counter receptionist.
– Reliance on Administrative Input Verification: Baseline system data
and notification accuracy remain heavily dependent on human entry. If a
receptionist inputs an invalid mobile number during walk-in check-in, the
automated “Get Ready” notification trigger will fail to route to the patient due
to a lack of built-in identity cross-checking links (such as CNIC biometric
validation).
